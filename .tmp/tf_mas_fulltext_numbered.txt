00015: Abstract
00016: The Mamba-type neural networks have gained significant popularity recently. To
00017: effectively and efficiently establish model architectures of Mamba, it is natural
00018: to introduce Neural Architecture Search (NAS) methods into Mamba. However,
00019: existing NAS methods tailored for Mamba are training-based, leading to substantial
00020: time and computational resource expenditure. To address this issue, and considering
00021: that Mamba2 is an improved version of the original Mamba, we propose a training-
00022: free NAS method specifically designed for Mamba2. Based on rank collapse in
00023: stacked State Space Duality (SSD) blocks, we design a proxy that only requires
00024: the computation of the transformation matrix and its gradient between two tensors
00025: within the network. Additionally, we develop a corresponding search space and
00026: introduce a novel approach for determining adjustable hyperparameter ranges.
00027: Experimental results show that our method outperforms all existing training-free
00028: NAS approaches in terms of both ranking correlation and the performance of search
00029: results for Mamba2 architecture. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
00030: training-free NAS method designed for Mamba-type architectures. Our codes are
00031: available at https://github.com/fanyi-plus/tf-nas.
00032: 1 Introduction
00033: Recently, the introduction of Mamba has provided new impetus for research in State Space Models
00034: (SSM) [24], and Mamba-type models possess potential to partially replace Transformer [61, 64, 29].
00035: Subsequently, Mamba2 [13], developed as an enhancement of the original Mamba, further improves
00036: network performance. However, similar to other types of networks, the practical use of Mamba-
00037: type networks still necessitates the manual specification of hyperparameters to determine the actual
00038: model architecture [14]. This process often involves trial-and-error, which can be time-consuming
00039: and labor-intensive. Therefore, it is logical to apply Neural Architecture Search (NAS) techniques
00040: [52] to the design of Mamba-type architectures. Currently, among the limited work, [50] explores
00041: hyperparameters such as network depth and width, while [27, 19] search for scanning orders of image
00042: patches for vision-oriented Mamba.
00043: However, these methods rely heavily on extensive training of the networks during the search process.
00044: In fact, recent research in the NAS is increasingly leaning towards training-free NAS [35], also known

00049: as zero-shot NAS. These approaches avoid the need to train candidate networks and instead compute
00050: a proxy relying only on information from the parameters, gradients, latent variables, etc., of the
00051: network in its initial state. The proxy is correlated with the actual performance of the network. The
00052: earliest training-free NAS methods target convolutional networks [31, 49, 28], followed by methods
00053: designed for Transformers [68, 54, 20]. Among these, DSS [68] is a classic method that originates
00054: from a finding suggesting that when stacking increasing attention layers in a model, the rank of the
00055: output trends towards 1 [18]. Based on this, DSS proposes an indirect measurement of the degree to
00056: which the rank approaches 1 as a representation of candidate network performance. Later adaptations
00057: of the method [69] introduce layer-wise proxies. Unfortunately, no training-free NAS methods for
00058: Mamba-type networks have yet emerged.
00059: To fill this gap in training-free NAS for Mamba-type networks, we propose TF-MAS. Given that
00060: the overall performance of typical Mamba-type model, Mamba2 [13], exceeds that of raw Mamba,
00061: our search space is designed based on Mamba2. By theoretically demonstrating that when the core
00062: structure, State Space Duality (SSD), in Mamba2 is stacked continuously, its output also trends
00063: towards 1. Inspired by DSS, we design a training-free NAS method for Mamba2. In this method, we
00064: need to compute the transformation matrix between the input tensor of each Mamba2 block and the
00065: input tensor of the corresponding SSD. When there are different relationships between the sequence
00066: length and feature dimensions of the input tensor of the Mamba2 block, we devise distinct methods
00067: to derive the transformation matrices. Once the transformation matrices are obtained, the values
00068: of the proxies can be computed. Additionally, we design a search space that includes 4 Adjustable
00069: Hyperparameters (AHs). Unlike existing methods, the ranges of these AHs are not artificially set
00070: but are determined through precise calculations based on the expected cost for specific tasks. This
00071: increases the likelihood of discovering candidate networks with superior performance within the
00072: search space.
00073: We construct several NASBenches based on existing pre-trained Mamba2 models, with each dataset
00074: containing 500 network architectures sampled from the specific search space along with their corre-
00075: sponding performance metrics on different datasets. We compute the ranking correlation between
00076: our proxies and actual performance on these NASBenches. The results indicate that our method
00077: outperforms all other training-free NAS approaches. Subsequently, under the overhead constraint, we
00078: establish the search space, conduct searches, and train the search results from scratch. The search
00079: results exceed the performance of the original Mamba2 while maintaining computational costs not
00080: greater than those of the original networks. These results demonstrate the unique effectiveness of our
00081: method in searching for Mamba2 architectures. Furthermore, we conduct additional experiments to
00082: investigate rank convergence, transformation matrices, and search spaces to validate the rationality
00083: and robustness of our method.
00084: We give an brief introduction about the related work and a discussion about societal impacts in Section
00085: A and B of the technical appendix, respectively. The contributions of this paper are as follows:
00086: • We introduce, for the first time, a training-free NAS method for the Mamba-type networks.
00087: • We design a search space where the range of AHs is dictated by computational costs.
00088: • We validate the effectiveness of our method through ranking correlation and the performance
00089: of search results.

00140: 3 Method
00141: 3.1 Proxy design
00142: [18] has been shown that if multiple attention modules are concatenated without the inclusion of
00143: regularization layers, the rank of the output during forward propagation will converge to 1 as the
00144: number of attention modules approaches infinity, a phenomenon known as rank collapse. Furthermore,
00145: [13] points out that the forward process of the attention module is essentially consistent with that of
00146: the SSD module, differing only in the representation of each variable. Therefore, we can reason that
00147: stacking multiple SSD modules will also result in rank collapse. A theoretical proof is provided in
00148: Subsection C.1 of the technical appendix, and we also verify this by experiments in Subsection 4.4.
00149: This phenomenon will affect the expressiveness of the network. This can be regarded as a reason
00150: why the Mamba2 network necessitates the use of regularization layers (see Figure 1). However, as
00151: described in [18], regularization layers can only partially mitigate rank collapse and cannot entirely
00152: eliminate it. Therefore, inspired by the evaluation method for Multi-head Self-Attention (MSA) in
00153: ZeroLM [69], we consider designing a new proxy to evaluate the performance of different Mamba2
00154: models by measuring the rank collapse magnitude.
00155: ZeroLM states that rank collapse magnitude of outputs can be measured by calculating the rank of
00156: the weights after network initialization. To reduce the computational overhead of the proxy, further
00157: theoretical analysis has transformed this into computing the product of the nuclear norm of the
00158: weights and the nuclear norm of the gradients of the weights. In attention module, the weights are
00159: typically the transformation matrices from input to query/key/value via a fully connected layer, while
00160: in Mamba2 block, the operations from U to X, B, and C are not merely a fully connected layer;
00161: they also involve convolution and SiLU. Here, we represent the transformation matrices fromU to
00162: X, B, and C in Mamba2 block as W X, W B, and W C, thus the sizes are W × P, W × N, and
00163: W × N, respectively. Due to the existence of convolution and SiLU, W X, W B, and W C do not
00164: equal to the weight of the fully connected layer. Consequently, to compute the score of the network,

00261: 3.2 Search space
00262: The design of the search space generally follows the design principles outlined in [10]. Specifically,
00263: we have set the following 4 AHs:
00264: • Depth (D): the number of Mamba2 blocks.
00265: • Width (W): the dimension of each token.
00266: • State dimension (N): the dimensionality of the state space.
00267: • Number of heads ( H): the number of heads in each Mamba2 block.
00268: All 4 AHs are positive integers. We set the dimension of each head to 64, which is consistent with the
00269: original Mamba2. This indicates that the number of columns in X is 64H. We denote this search
00270: space as SSMamba2 (Search Space of Mamba2). Moreover, to enhance the flexibility of the network
00271: architecture, and inspired by [10], we extend SSMamba2 to form VWSSMamba2 (Variable Width
00272: Search Space of Mamba2). The candidate networks within this search space differ from existing
00273: Mamba2; thus, we refer to them as VWMamba2 (Variable Width of Mamba2). This design permits
00274: the values of N and H to differ across layers, meaning that for a specific network, N and H are no
00275: longer solely positive integers but rather D-dimensional vectors, where the i-th element represents
00276: the values of N and H in the i-th layer. It should be noted that our work follows the same protocol as
00277: [18] and uniformly employs skip connections in all candidate networks.
00278: The range of values for each AH is determined by the expected computational cost. Suppose the
00279: expected computational overhead is provided by an upper limit of parameter number, denoted by
00280: c0. When constructing SSMamba2, we will reference an existing Mamba2 model, referred to as the
00281: reference model2, to construct a virtual benchmark model. This benchmark model must satisfy two
00282: conditions: (a) According to the scaling rule, we want this benchmark model to be a proportional
00283: scaling of the reference model. (b) The capacity of this benchmark model must equal c0.
00284: Let the AHs of the reference model be Dr, Wr, Nr, and Hr, and the AHs of the benchmark
00285: model be Db, Wb, Nb, and Hb. According to condition (a), the equations Db = kDr, Wb = kWr,
00286: Nb = kNr, and Hb = kHr (where k is a positive constant) should be satisfied. Besides, the
00287: parameter number of an existing Mamba2 model can be computed as W + 2VW + DW + 10DN +
00288: 387DH+2DWN+193DWH (calculation details can be found in Section E of the technical appendix),
00289: where V is the dictionary length, a predetermined value. According to condition (b), we have
00290: Wb + 2VWb + DbWb + 10DbNb + 387DbHb + 2DbWbNb + 193DbWbHb = c0. By substituting
00291: Db = kDr, Wb = kWr, Nb = kNr, and Hb = kHr into the equation and rearranging, we obtain a
00292: cubic equation in terms of k,
00293: (2DrWrNr + 193DrWrHr) k3 + (2VWr + DrWr + 387DrHr + 10DrNr) k2 + Wrk = c0. (7)
00294: This equation can be solved using the quadratic formula (it is clear that the equation has a unique
00295: solution). Once k is obtained, the AHs of the benchmark model can be determined. It is important
00296: to note that the AHs determined using this method are likely not integers; thus, we refer to this
00297: benchmark model as virtual. The benchmark model does not need to be physically constructed; it
00298: merely serves as a reference for determining the range of AHs. Ultimately, we will take⌈0.6× xb

00364: 4 Experiments
00365: 4.1 Ranking correlation
00366: The effectiveness of a proxy is most directly measured by ranking correlation. This involves sampling
00367: several network architectures from the search space, training them, testing their actual performance,
00368: and calculating their proxy values. The ranking correlation is just the correlation between the proxy
00369: values and the performance of the sampled architectures. A higher ranking correlation indicates that
00370: the proxy can more accurately estimate the performance of the network.
00371: To save computation time, some researchers have stored sampled architectures and their corresponding
00372: performances to create datasets known as NASBench [43]. To our knowledge, current NASBenches
00373: encompass only CNN [ 65, 17, 57, 16] and Transformer [ 62], with no one specific to Mamba.
00374: Therefore, we will sample architectures from both SSMamba2 and VWSSMamba2 to construct
00375: NASBenches pertaining to Mamba. However, training numerous Mamba2 networks comparable
00376: to the model sizes reported in [ 13] from scratch incurs a significant time cost. To mitigate this,
00377: we employ weight entanglement proposed in [ 10]. After sampling the architectures, we directly
00378: activate the corresponding part of parameters from Mamba2 pretrained models as the parameters
00379: of the sampled architectures. Nevertheless, we cannot guarantee that weight entanglement will
00380: function effectively under Mamba2. Therefore, for each sampled network, we perform 10 epoches of

00427: on VMSSMamba2Bench_2.7B. Kendall’s Tau is used as correlation in this paper. Section F of the
00428: technical appendix includes results for other NASBenches. We compare our method with other
00429: existing proxies, including the parameter number, which is often treated as a baseline [35]. Given
00430: that our method is the first known training-free NAS targeting the Mamba-type models, all other
00431: methods are oriented towards CNN or Transformer. It is worth noting that for attention map-based
00432: proxies such as Attention Confidence [54] and AttnNAS [20], we consider CB ⊤ as the attention map,
00433: motivated by the duality between attention and SSD. Additionally, some classical proxies require
00434: specific operations or structures within the network (e.g., the CNN-oriented NASWOT [44] requires
00435: ReLU), which are absent in Mamba2; therefore, we do not include comparisons with these proxies.
00436: It is apparent that the ranking correlation of existing methods is generally lower than the baseline,
00437: with only our method surpassing the baseline. This indicates that while existing methods have played
00438: a significant role in predicting the performance of CNNs and Transformers, they struggle to adapt to
00439: Mamba2, showcasing the value of our method. Furthermore, we observe an interesting phenomenon
00440: where Transformer-oriented methods (albeit not as effective as our proposed method) perform slightly
00441: better than CNN-oriented methods. We speculate that this may be due to certain structural similarities
00442: between Transformers and Mamba2, which provides inspiration for future improvements in proxies
00443: targeting Mamba2, suggesting an exploration of insights from existing Transformer-based proxies.
00444: Moreover, recent studies have highlighted that ranking correlation evaluated only on the top- k
00445: candidates can be particularly informative in certain scenarios [67, 15]. We therefore computed the
00446: corresponding metrics and obtained results consistent with those in Table 1 (see Section F of the
00447: technical appendix), demonstrating the robustness of our proxy.

00704: the type of all candidates are the same, we expect them to exhibit a similar level of test-variance.
00705: 5 Conclusion
00706: We propose the first training-free NAS method designed for the Mamba-type architectures. Based
00707: on the rank collapse in stacked SSD layers, we develop a proxy that requires computation of the
00708: transformation matrix between the input of the Mamba2 block and the input of the SSD block.
00709: Additionally, we design a search space where the ranges of the AHs can flexibly vary according to the
00710: expected computational overhead. We validate the effectiveness of our method from two perspectives,
00711: ranking correlation and the performance of search results. However, we have only applied our method
00712: on Mamba2 so far. In the future, as many new Mamba-type models are proposed, we will validate
00713: and optimize our method on a broader range of models.

01288: A Related work
01289: Significant progress has been made in the area of Mamba-related research recently. The Mamba
01290: model [24], as a novel and efficient sequence modeling framework based on SSM [25], has garnered
01291: widespread attention. Later, the introduction of Mamba2 [ 13] makes the performance improve
01292: by 2 to 8 times through enhancements in selective SSM. Mamba2 has demonstrated competitive
01293: performance in language modeling against Transformers and has optimized system performance by
01294: introducing tensor parallelism. Subsequently, Mamba-type model makes greater progress in vision
01295: and multimodal tasks, with excellent models such as Vision Mamba [41], SiMBA [48], VMRNN
01296: [59], and MambaMixer [5]. Additionally, foundation models like Falcon Mamba 7B [71], Jamba [37],
01297: VideoMamba [9] have been proposed as attempts to replace Transformer models across various tasks.
01298: However, some studies indicate that there is not a replacement relationship between Mamba and
01299: existing architectures; combining Mamba with current models like Transformer [22], convolutional
01300: network [23], graph network [4], recurrent network [6], spiking network [3] may lead to better results.
01301: These contributions have not only deepened theoretical understanding of Mamba but also propelled
01302: its development through system optimization and application expansion, revealing immense potential
01303: across multiple fields. However, many of these models involve numerous hyperparameters that dictate
01304: the actual architecture of the models and often require manual tuning.
01305: NAS is initially introduced by [70] and has since led to various optimization strategy-based methods
01306: [30, 2, 42, 63, 12]. Considering that these methods still require extensive computational resources
01307: during the search process, some researchers propose training-free NAS, also known as zero-shot NAS,
01308: inspired by parameter prunning [34, 58, 1]. Subsequently, grounded in neural network learning theory,
01309: multiple training-free NAS methods for convolution networks have been proposed by analyzing
01310: activation pattern [44, 49], analyzing gradients [56], utilizing neural tangent kernel [55, 11], viewing
01311: the performance prediction as a Gaussian process [47], or employ alternative heuristic methods [31].
01312: To achieve better performance, some studies [ 26, 28, 33] combine multiple proxies and employ
01313: optimization strategies to switch among them dynamically. Unfortunately, the design of proxies in
01314: these methods necessitates consideration of the characteristics of network structures and training
01315: principles, making them unsuitable for the prevalent Transformer architecture. To address this, DSS
01316: [68], based on the theoretical insights from [21, 18], pioneers a NAS method for Transformers that
01317: measures the synaptic diversity in MSA modules and synaptic significance in Multi-Layer Perceptron
01318: (MLP) modules. Building on this, DSS++ [ 69] proposes a layer-wise proxy approach. Other
01319: researchers [54, 20] have evaluated network performance using attention map features. In summary,
01320: current training-free NAS designs primarily focus on convolutional networks and Transformers,
01321: leaving other architectures, including Mamba, without suitable methods. From another perspective,
01322: the existing methods for optimizing Mamba-type models [ 50, 27, 19] all utilize traditional NAS
01323: approaches, which involve significant computational overhead. Therefore, research on training-free
01324: NAS for Mamba-type models is highly urgent.

01458: C.3 Probability of full-rank matrix for Gaussian random matrix
01459: In this subsection, we will prove that, for a matrix sampled from an independent standard normal
01460: distribution, the probability of it being full rank is 1.
01461: First, consider the case where the matrix is square. When the matrix is an n × n square matrix,
01462: its determinant is a continuous and non-constant function that maps the elements of the matrix
01463: to real numbers. The set of singular matrices (those with a determinant equal to zero) forms a
01464: low-dimensional algebraic variety. According to the properties of absolutely continuous probability
01465: measures, this set has Lebesgue measure zero. Therefore, the probability that a matrix with indepen-
01466: dently and identically distributed standard normal elements lies in this zero-measure set is zero. Thus,
01467: the probability that the matrix is of full rank is 1.
01468: Next, we consider the case of non-square matrices. For a non-square matrix A (assumed to be of size
01469: m × n), if m > n , the rank of the matrix is at most n. Conversely, if m < n , the rank is at most m.
01470: However, for matrices whose elements are drawn from independent standard normal distributions, the
01471: row (or column) vectors are linearly independent with probability 1. When m = n, the probability
01472: that the matrix is of full rank is 1; when m < n , the probability that the matrix has full row rank is
01473: also 1; similarly, when m > n , the probability that the matrix has full column rank is 1.
01474: Additionally, we can use mathematical induction for proof. Assume the probability that the first
01475: k row vectors are linearly independent is 1. When we add the (k + 1)-th row, the probability that
01476: this row falls within the k-dimensional subspace spanned by the previous k rows is zero. Given the
01477: independence of the standard normal distribution, the probability that the newly added row vector is
01478: linearly dependent on the original set of row vectors is zero. Therefore, by mathematical induction,
01479: we conclude that the probability of all row vectors being linearly independent is 1, which implies that
01480: the probability of the matrix being full rank is also 1.
01481: C.4 Probability of inclusion in the column space of row-full rank matrices
01482: Let A be an m × n matrix satisfying m < n and having full row rank (i.e., its rank is m). In this
01483: section, we will verify that the probability of a random vector x ∈ Rm, sampled from an independent
01484: standard normal distribution, lying within the column space of A is 1.
01485: Since A has full row rank (rank-m), its row space has dimension m, and consequently, its column
01486: space also has dimension m. We denote the column space of A as Col (A). Given that A is an
01487: m × n matrix, the column space Col (A) is a subspace of Rm. Moreover, a full row rank matrix will
01488: have a column space dimension of m, which matches the dimension of Rm. Therefore, we have
01489: Col (A) = Rm. The random vector x is independently sampled from a standard normal distribution,
01490: which means its distribution covers the entire Rm space, except for a set of measure zero. In Rm,
01491: the measure of single-point sets (such as the origin) or lower-dimensional subspaces is zero. Since
01492: Col (A) = Rm, the probability that x lies in Col (A) is therefore 1. Thus, we have proven this
01493: conclusion.

02243: In Subsection 4.4, we introduce a metric to evaluate the range of AHs within the search space.
02244: This section will elaborate on the details of this metric. Intuitively, an ideal AH range needs to
02245: ensure that as many architectures as possible with their parameter numbers fall within the interval
02246: [0.9c0, c0], while also maintaining diversity among these architectures by maximizing their pairwise
