LLMENAS: Evolutionary Neural Architecture
Search via Large Language Model Guidance
Yutao Lai, Zicheng Cai, Lei Chen, Tongtao Ling, Hai-Lin Liu
Abstract—Differentiable Neural Architecture Search (NAS)
and traditional evolutionary approaches frequently struggle
with premature convergence to local optima. To overcome this
limitation, we propose LLMENAS, a hierarchical framework
that introduces trajectory-aware fitness design as the upper-
level optimizer. By analyzing the convergence state of historical
optimization trajectories, the Large Language Model (LLM) acts
as a fitness designer to dynamically design fitness functions. This
mechanism enables the search to navigate complex landscapes
and escape local optima. Furthermore, we introduce a closed-
loop self-improving mechanism, enabling the LLM to iteratively
enhance its design strategies through self-reflection and self-
refinement based on feedback. Extensive experiments show that
LLMENAS achieves competitive results, with top-1 accuracies
of 97.58% on CIFAR-10, 83.52% on CIFAR-100, and 75.6%
on ImageNet-1k. Furthermore, it is achieved with remarkable
efficiency, costing only 0.15 GPU days on the CIFAR 10 datasets
and 2 GPU days on ImageNet. The source code is publicly
available at: https://github.com/LLMENAS/LLMENAS.
Index Terms—Neural architecture search (NAS), Evolutionary
strategy, Large language model, image classification.
I. INTRODUCTION
F
Ueled by the exponential growth in computing power,
deep convolutional neural networks have experienced
remarkable advancements over the past decade. The design
of neural architectures has emerged as a prominent research
focus, with various architectures [1]–[4] proposed by re-
searchers being widely applied in fields such as image recogni-
tion, natural language processing, and semantic segmentation.
However, as neural network architectures grow deeper and
more complex, designing and fine-tuning these networks has
become increasingly challenging. Traditional neural network
design often requires substantial human effort, expertise, and
specialized knowledge, posing significant barriers to efficiency
and scalability.
The emergence of Neural Architecture Search (NAS) has
been precisely aimed at addressing this challenge. By au-
tomating the exploration of network architectures in the well-
established search space, NAS is capable of discovering high-
quality architectures that often exhibit superior performance
and efficiency for specific tasks. Therefore, NAS has emerged

as a focal point within the deep learning community, particu-
larly in applications such as image classification [5], [6], image
segmentation [7], [8], and object detection [9], [10]. NAS
methods are broadly categorized into three main approaches:
evolutionary algorithms-based, reinforcement learning-based,
and gradient-based. Recently, studies on neural network search
have primarily focused on evolutionary algorithms based NAS
and Gradient-Based NAS. EA-based NAS methods [11], [12]
incur heavy search costs. To this end, following the introduc-
tion of Differentiable Architecture Search (DARTS), gradient-
based NAS has seen significant development and attention.
DARTS has made neural network architecture search more
efficient and flexible by introducing a differentiable search
space. This approach allows for the training and evaluation of
various candidate network structures within a weight-sharing
supernet, substantially reducing the complexity and compu-
tational cost of the search. However, some flaws of DARTS
have gradually been revealed by researchers, among which the
most significant issue is falling into local optima [13], [14]. In
DARTS, the architecture parameters are tightly coupled with
the supernet weights. During the search process in DARTS,
this coupling can lead to a performance collapse phenomenon,
leading to a sharp decline in network performance. This
phenomenon typically occurs when the algorithm tends to
select parameter-free operations during the search process,
such as skip connections, thereby leading to getting stuck in
local optima. The mainstream strategy in LLM-based NAS
methods [15], [16] positions the LLM as a direct architecture
generator, an approach that has opened up novel avenues
in the field. However, it also suffers from some limitations.
First, its low output diversity often traps the search process in
local optima. Second, in complex search spaces, the LLM’s
inherent propensity for hallucination can yield invalid archi-
tectures. Combining the strength of evolutionary algorithms
with the efficiency of gradient-based methods presents a highly
promising direction [13], [17], [18]. However, the effectiveness
of combining these methods requires careful coordination of
exploration and exploitation during the search process.
To transcend these limitations, this paper proposes LLME-
NAS, a general hierarchical optimization framework. Unlike
traditional static formulations, we formulate the upper-level
optimization as a trajectory-aware fitness design process. By
analyzing the historical optimization trajectories, the LLM
acts as a fitness designer to identify when the lower-level
evolutionary search is trapped in a local optimum. It then
generates fitness functions to dynamically reshape the search
landscape, effectively smoothing out local barriers and guiding
the population toward promising regions.

The specific contributions of this paper are summarized as
follows:
1) We propose a general hierarchical optimization frame-
work that employs a trajectory-aware LLM as the upper-
level fitness designer. Leveraging its powerful reasoning

II. RELATED WORK
In this section, we will provide an overview of relevant work
in NAS and review approaches to NAS based on LLMs.
A. Neural Architecture search
Since the advent of deep learning, the design of neu-
ral network architectures has played a pivotal role in en-
hancing model performance, exemplified by breakthroughs
like ResNet [3]. However, crafting these architectures typi-
cally demands substantial human effort, resources, and expert
knowledge. To overcome these challenges, Neural Architecture
Search has emerged as a promising solution. As a subfield of
AutoML, NAS aims to automate the design of neural network
architectures, significantly reducing the reliance on manual
intervention. Within the broader AutoML domain, NAS has
remained a highly active and influential area of research.
Overall, existing neural architecture search methods can
be categorized into three types based on their search strate-
gies: reinforcement learning-based neural architecture search
(RL-based NAS), gradient-based neural architecture search
(GD-based NAS), and evolutionary algorithm-based neural
architecture search (EA-based NAS). RL-based NAS employs
reinforcement learning techniques to guide the search process,
typically using a controller to generate neural network archi-
tectures and rewarding it based on the performance of the
generated models. GD-based NAS leverages gradient descent
to optimize the architecture by differentiating through the
search space, enabling efficient and continuous optimization
of neural network architectures. EA-based NAS refers to the
use of evolutionary algorithms to search and optimize the
architecture and hyperparameters of neural networks.
RL-based NAS models the neural architecture search as a
Markov decision process, treating the generation of network
architectures as an agent’s action-selection process. [19] is
a pioneering work in neural architecture search, using rein-
forcement learning to discover the optimal network architec-
ture, achieving comparable performance to human-designed
networks. Ding et al. [20] proposed an RL-based NAS method
that utilizes a broad scalable architecture called BCNN (Broad
Convolutional Neural Network), which addresses the issue
of slow propagation speed in ENAS, which arises from its
deep network topology. Specifically, BNAS incorporates the
reinforcement learning-based search strategy and parameter
sharing from ENAS but applies them to the newly designed
BCNN. To address the challenge of selecting a suitable autoen-
coder architecture, Dissem et al. [21] utilized a reinforcement
learning-based neural architecture search (RLNAS) approach
to discover the optimal neural configuration.
Gradient-based neural architecture search methods trans-
form the discrete search space into a continuous one by
relaxing the search parameters. all candidate operations are
represented in DARTS [5] as a supernet. DARTS relaxes
the discrete architecture parameters into a continuous form,
allowing for the simultaneous optimization of both the archi-
tecture parameters and model weights, significantly reducing
the search costs for neural architecture search while achieving
considerable performance. Although gradient-based methods
have significantly improved the efficiency of neural architec-
ture search, they still have limitations in certain aspects. Due
to the gradient-based approach’s preference for parameter-less
operations such as skip connections, DARTS often falls into
local optima [22], [23] during the architecture search process,
leading to a significant performance collapse. To address this
performance collapse issue, many research efforts have been
proposed [24], [25]. Some approaches focus on improving the
optimization during the search process by introducing regu-
larization, early stopping [26], [27]. Other works explore the
way to more accurately evaluate the importance of candidate
operations [28], [29].
EA-based NAS refers to the use of evolutionary algorithms
to search and optimize the architecture and hyperparameters of
neural networks. EA-based NAS has become one of the most
widely used strategies for discovering optimal neural network
architectures. LargeEvo [11] is the first attempt to apply EA to
execute a neural architecture search. LargeEvo employs sim-
ple mutation operations on individual candidates has yielded
highly competitive neural network architectures on the CIFAR-
10 and CIFAR-100 datasets. Sun et al. [30] introduced an
EA-based approach to search a predefined search space for
neural architecture search. Furthermore, the paper incorporates
skip connections to mitigate the issue of vanishing gradients.
However, the high search cost associated with EA-based NAS
methods has attracted considerable criticism, becoming one of
the main bottlenecks. To address this issue, various strategies
have been applied in NAS to reduce the computational cost
of EA-based NAS. Additionally, many studies [8], [31], [32]
have been conducted utilizing surrogate models to reduce
the computational expense associated with EA-based neural
network architecture search. Some studies have successfully
reduced computational costs by employing weight inheritance
techniques. For example, Huang et al. proposed an elite weight

B. LLM-based Optimization
LLMs [36]–[38] are models trained to predict the next word
in a sequence. Their power comes from three core ingredients:
data, scale, and transformer architecture. LLMs have recently
undergone revolutionary breakthroughs, which demonstrate
unprecedented capabilities across diverse fields, including code
generation [39] and natural language processing [40]. Notably,
they also exhibit strong potential in advanced cognitive tasks
such as automated algorithm design [41]–[43]. Such progress
is considered a significant step towards Artificial General
Intelligence (AGI). For example, Liu et al. [41], [44] combined
evolutionary computation with LLM-based automated algo-
rithm design, achieving competitive results on several com-
monly used combinatorial optimization benchmark datasets.
Above LLM-based heuristic evolution methods often converge
prematurely to local optima. To counteract this limitation,
researchers have actively explored various strategies [45]–
[47] aimed at promoting population diversity. In addition,
researchers have explored the use of large language models for
mathematical discovery [48]–[50]. For example, the recently
proposed FunSearch [48], using an evolutionary procedure that
pairs a pre-trained LLM with a systematic evaluator, discov-
ered new constructions of larger cap sets. Novikov et al. [49]
proposed an evolutionary coding agent, namely AlphaEvolve,
that significantly enhances state-of-the-art LLM capabilities
for challenging tasks like scientific problem-solving and com-
putational infrastructure optimization by autonomously having
LLMs directly modify code.
While Evolutionary Algorithms (EAs) are widely valued for
their global search in black-box optimization, their efficacy is
often hampered by a reliance on random exploration and a
lack of task-specific knowledge, leading to inefficient searches
and slow convergence. The advent of LLMs, pre-trained on
vast and diverse datasets, offers a compelling solution; their
rich, implicitly learned cross-domain knowledge and abstract
patterns can provide crucial heuristic guidance to EAs. [51]
LLMs can guide population initialization [44], direct the
selection or generation of search operators [52], [53], or be
directly employed as optimizers [54], [55]. Large language
models have also been integrated into surrogate-assisted evo-
lutionary algorithms [56], [57], yielding highly competitive
experimental results. This integration enhances the efficiency
and effectiveness of EAs.
C. LLM-based Neural Architecture Search
Given that LLMs possess an exceptional capability for
understanding and generating neural network architectures,
they have been employed as neural network generators within
a framework combined with Evolutionary Algorithm (EA)
search, achieving competitive experimental results. Zheng
et al. [58] introduced an innovative approach called GPT-
4 Enhanced Neural Architecture Search (GENIUS), which
harnesses the generative power of GPT-4 as a black-box opti-
mizer to search promising architectures. Chen et al. [16] pro-
posed combining evolutionary prompt engineering with soft
prompt tuning, which consistently identifies diverse and high-
performing models. Nasir et al. [15] proposed a method called
LLMatic, which combines the generative capabilities of LLMs
with the diversity and robustness of Quality Diversity (QD)
algorithms to search for optimal architectures. Zhou et al. [59]
proposed a Large Language Model-Assisted Design Principle
Transfer (LAPT) framework to enhance the efficiency and per-
formance of Transferable Neural Architecture Search (TNAS).
Ji et al. [60] proposed the Reflective Zero-cost NAS (RZ-NAS)
method, which leverages training-free metrics within a zero-
cost framework to improve the capabilities of LLM for NAS.
Yang et al. [61] introduce NADER, a novel framework that
reframes neural architecture design as a large-language-model-
based multi-agent collaboration task. Fang et al. proposed
the Personalized Federated Graph Neural Architecture Search
(PFGNAS) method, which leverages task-specific prompts to
continuously discover and integrate optimal GNN architectures
within a federated learning framework.

designed fitness function.

C. Evolutionary Strategy for Neural Architecture Search
Recent research has highlighted that differentiability-based
methods for neural network architecture search are prone
to falling into local optima [14], [29], which can lead to
performance collapse. To address this challenge, this paper
adopts Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy [64]
(CMA-ES) to explore better evolutionary paths to avoid pre-
mature convergence during the search process. Compared to
differentiability-based methods, CMA-ES can more effectively
explore a broader range of paths. Leveraging sophisticated
sampling strategies and adaptive covariance matrix adjust-
ments, CMA-ES conducts a deeper and more comprehensive
search across the complex landscape of neural network archi-
tectures. This capability significantly increases the likelihood
of identifying promising paths and escaping local minima.

F . Generated Output Verification Mechanism
To ensure the reliability of the outputs automatically gen-
erated by the LLM, a verification mechanism [39], [53] is
essential. When the LLM generates a candidate output, these
outputs are subjected to a specific verification process. For
generated code for fitness functions, this typically involves
automated checks like linting and syntax validation, followed
by execution with predefined test instances to assess its
functional correctness and stability.
In instances where the generated outputs fail this verifica-
tion, a generated output verification prompt is triggered. This
prompt requires LLM to regenerate the new outputs, providing
context about the specific error encountered and reiterating the
original requirements and constraints.
This verification mechanism is iterative: the newly gen-
erated output undergoes the same verification process. This
loop continues until the generated code successfully passes
all verification stages or a predefined maximum number of
regeneration attempts, denoted asK, is reached. If, afterK
attempts, a valid fitness function has not been successfully
generated and verified, we default to a robust fitness function
f=L1and a corresponding default idea as a fallback
mechanism to ensure the continuity of the evolutionary search.

A. Datasets
Both CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets consist of 60,000
images, with each image having a resolution of 32x32 pixels.
The CIFAR-10 dataset is comprised of 10 classes, whereas
CIFAR-100 contains 100 classes. The ImageNet dataset is a
relatively large dataset consisting of 1,000 classes. It contains
1,100,000 images in the training set and 50,000 images in the
testing set. In the ImageNet dataset, each image maintains a
resolution of 224 * 224 pixels. To rapidly assess architectural
performance, Dong et al. [82] introduced NAS-Bench-201,
a benchmark dataset for neural network architecture search.
It comprises 15,625 neural cell candidate architectures, each
trained multiple times across CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and
ImageNet16-120 datasets, with training logs and performance
metrics provided for every training. In NAS-Bench-201, the
training logs and performance metrics for the candidate archi-
tectures are provided, eliminating the need for researchers to
train them from scratch.
B. Implementation Details
1) CIFAR-10/100:Following the search space of DARTS,
we adopt the same operation spaceOin this paper. In order
to reduce memory usage and search cost, the partial channel
connection strategy proposed in PC-DARTS is employed in
this study. During the search stage, the CIFAR-10 dataset was
meticulously split into two equal parts. One part was utilized
for architecture search, while the other was employed for
optimizing the supernet weights. For the neural architecture
search on the CIFAR-10 dataset, we executed a 50 epochs
search, with the first 15 epochs designated as a warm-up
phase, using a batch size of 256. The architecture derived from
this search was then meticulously retrained for 600 epochs
from scratch. In terms of parameter configuration for CMA-
ES, the population size was set to 25, with an initial standard
deviation of 0.05, and all other parameters were aligned with
the standard CMA-ES settings. The step size and the initial
number of channels were set to 0.8 and 16, respectively.
2) ImageNet:The ImageNet dataset is vast and diverse,
making it significantly more complex than CIFAR-10. For
the neural architecture search on the ImageNet dataset, we
conducted a 50 epochs search, with the first 25 epochs
designated as a warm-up phase, using a batch size of 1024. The
architecture derived from this search was then meticulously
retrained for 600 epochs from scratch. In terms of parameter
configuration for CMA-ES, the population size was set to
50, with an initial standard deviation of 0.05, and all other
parameters were aligned with the standard CMA-ES settings.
The step size and the initial number of channels were set to
0.8 and 16, respectively.
3) NAS-Bench-201:In the NAS-Bench-201 benchmark, all
candidate architectures have been evaluated. Therefore, the
performance metrics of architectures obtained through neural
architecture search can be obtained directly. For more details
regarding NAS-Bench-201, please refer to [82].

G. Underlying Mechanism Analysis
From an optimization perspective, the proposed LLM-
guided fitness design can be interpreted as anadaptive regular-
ization mechanismacting on the evolutionary search process.
Rather than explicitly modifying gradients or network param-
eters, the LLM intervenes at the fitness-construction level,
dynamically reshaping the effective optimization landscape
encountered by the evolutionary algorithm. This higher-level
intervention allows the search process to adjust its inductive
bias in response to trajectory-level signals such as stagnation,
instability, or premature convergence.
In deep learning theory [90], [91], flat minima are widely
associated with robustness and improved generalization, as
solutions located in wide basins exhibit reduced sensitivity
to parameter perturbations. This perspective has been explic-
itly emphasized in the neural architecture search literature,
particularly by R-DARTS [88] and SDARTS [89], which
highlight that the geometry of the convergence point is critical
for optimization stability. Specifically, architectural weights
are expected to converge to a flat minimum, defined as a
region of the loss landscape where predictive performance
remains insensitive to small parameter perturbations. Within
LLMENAS, such regions are implicitly promoted through
adaptive modulation of selection pressure. When the popu-
lation is trapped in sharp local optima, the LLM reduces its
relative advantage via fitness reformulation strategies. These
operations effectively flatten narrow basins and encourage
exploration toward wider and more stable regions of the search
space.
The LLM leverages trajectory-level information extracted
from historical optimization processes to approximate when

H. Ablation Study
1) Analysis of the Number of Skip-Connections:To further
investigate whether the search avoids degenerate local optima,
we tracked the evolution of skip-connections during the search
process. A well-known failure mode in differentiable NAS
(e.g., DARTS) is the collapse into parameter-less operations
(like skip-connections) because they offer a quick path to
lower loss without learning valid features.
As shown in Figure 2, the DARTS baseline (blue line)
quickly collapses, saturating at the maximum of four skip-
connections. Similarly, the fixed fitness function baseline
(red line) becomes trapped, stabilizing at three. Even the
adaptive fitness baseline (green line) accumulates excessive
skip-connections over time. In contrast, LLMENAS (orange
line) consistently maintains a low and stable number of
skip-connections throughout the search. This proves that the
trajectory-aware fitness strategyπ LLM successfully identifies
and penalizes the ”path of least resistance.” By dynamically
reshaping the fitness function when structural homogeneity is
detected, LLMENAS forces the optimizer to explore complex
operations (e.g., convolutions) that hold greater long-term
potential, effectively preventing structural collapse.
2)Impact of Components in the Proposed Method:To
assess the contributions of each component in the proposed
LLMENAS to the experimental performance, we conducted
two ablation studies: (1) LLMENAS-V1, where we disabled
the LLM self-improving mechanism; (2) LLMENAS-V2,
where we disabled the fitness function designed by the LLM.
The experiments were conducted on the CIFAR-10 dataset,
where all obtained architectures were subsequently retrained
for evaluation. As evidenced by the Table VI, disabling any
component within the proposed algorithm leads to a no-
table decline in the performance of the architectures obtained
through the search process. In LLMENAS-V1, disabling the
LLM self-improving mechanism resulted in an inability to
adaptively refine the search strategy based on past proposed
strategy. Consequently, the algorithm’s ability to converge to
optimal regions within the search space was hindered, leading
to a decline in performance. The impact of disabling the
LLM-designed fitness function in LLMENAS-V2 is far more
pronounced, with the test error increasing significantly to
3.19%. This highlights its crucial role in the search process.
A significant reduction is also observed in the parameter
count of the discovered architecture, which decreased from
3.5M in our full method to 2.6M. This is not a benefit but
rather a clear symptom of failed exploration. Without the
guidance from the LLM-designed fitness function, the search
process defaults to an elite selection strategy. Such a strategy
is highly susceptible to converging on local optima, in this
case, overly simple and shallow architectures. These small
models are easy to optimize for quick marginal gains, but lack
the architectural complexity and capacity required for high
performance.
In contrast, the full LLMENAS framework uses the LLM
to create a more robust fitness function that balances ex-
ploitation with exploration. This guidance promotes a broader
exploration of the search space, thereby mitigating the risk
of the search collapsing into a local optimum. The resulting
3.5M parameter model is a good example of a more effective
search that successfully explored complex regions to find
an outstanding and high-capacity architecture. Therefore, the
LLM-designed fitness function is essential to escape local
optima and discover truly novel and effective architectures.
3)Impact of Key HyperparametersTandδon Per-
formance:To analyze the impact of the LLM-based fitness
design frequency T on experimental performance, this paper
conducted comparative experiments on six versions with T =
1, 3, 5, 10, 15, 20 on the CIFAR-10 dataset, as shown in
the Fig . 3. We conducted a thorough analysis to investigate
the impact of the LLM-based fitness function’s invocation
frequency, controlled by the hyperparameter T, which denotes
the interval in epochs between LLM queries. As illustrated in
our experiments, a clear trade-off emerges between validation
accuracy and the associated search cost. Our results indicate
that the most frequent invocation setting, T=1, achieves the
highest validation accuracy of 86.15%. While this frequent
querying entails a higher computational load, we found the
increase in search cost to be modest. Considering our primary
goal of maximizing the search algorithm’s performance, we
determined that this substantial gain in accuracy justifies
the minor increase in computational overhead. Therefore, we
selected T=1 as the default value in our study, as it achieves the
best performance without incurring a prohibitive search cost.
To analyze the impact of the hyperparameterδ, which controls
the influence of prior knowledge on the fitness function based
on LLM. For this, we conducted comparative experiments
on the CIFAR-10 dataset withδset to{1,3,5,10,20}, and
the results are shown in Figure 4. The analysis reveals that
the effectiveness of the prior knowledge is sensitive to the
value ofδ. Both low values (e.g.,δ= 1) and high values
(e.g.,δ= 10,20) resulted in lower validation accuracy. The
performance peaked atδ= 5, achieving the highest validation
accuracy of86.15%. Crucially, this peak in performance does
not come at the highest computational cost. While the search
cost forδ= 5(approximately0.15GPU days) is on par with
that forδ= 3, it is lower than the cost forδ= 1. Given that it
achieves the best accuracy with a reasonable search cost, we
setδ= 5as the default value for our study.

V. CONCLUSION ANDFUTUREWORK
In this paper, we introduced LLMENAS, a general hierar-
chical optimization framework that transcends the limitations
of traditional static NAS formulations. By positioning the
Large Language Model as a trajectory-aware fitness designer.
This hierarchical interaction, empowered by a closed-loop
self-improving mechanism, effectively smoothes out local bar-
riers and guides the evolutionary search toward promising
regions. Extensive experiments demonstrate that LLMENAS
not only achieves state-of-the-art performance across CIFAR-
10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet benchmarks but does so with
remarkable efficiency (0.15 GPU days), validating that se-
mantic reasoning can be seamlessly integrated into numerical
optimization with negligible latency.
Limitation and Future Work:The inclusion of historical
code and strategies provides the necessary semantic context.
However, we acknowledge a limitation inherent in the current
input format. The LLM’s reliance on a scalar trajectory (δ-step
loss) restricts it to phenomenological reasoning, precluding
access to high-dimensional internal states such as architecture
topology. Nevertheless, as our experiments demonstrated, this
abstraction is sufficient for effectively navigating the evolving
search landscape, so we reserve the integration of richer
state representations (to enable mechanistic reasoning) for
future work. The decision-making process of LLMs lacks
the transparency inherent in traditional methods. We aim to
address this by advancing our theoretical analysis to enhance
the interpretability of the proposed approach in the future.
In addition, we will include the extension of this framework
to multi-objective optimization scenarios and investigating the
application of the proposed method in other large-scale real-
world tasks.
