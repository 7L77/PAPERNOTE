Abstract—Neural Architecture Search (NAS) aims to automate
the design of neural networks, enabling the discovery of highly
effective architectures. Recent advancements in NAS have shown
significant success in identifying high-performing Transformer
architectures for computer vision and natural language process-
ing tasks. However, most NAS research has focused on supervised
learning frameworks, which rely heavily on labeled data. This
dependence on labeled data makes deploying these methods in
real-world applications challenging due to the high cost of data
annotation. Additionally, previous studies often prioritize model
performance while neglecting generalization ability, particularly
in scenarios with limited labeled data. To address these chal-
lenges, this study introduces a generalization-aware zero-shot
proxy based on self-supervised learning. By combining this proxy
with a complementary zero-shot proxy, we identify architectures
that balance generalization ability and expressivity. Experimental
results demonstrate that the architectures discovered using the
proposed approach achieve competitive performance on the
ImageNet and Wikitext-2 datasets while significantly reducing
the required labeled data by up to 75% and 99%, respectively.
Index Terms —Neural Architecture Search, Self-Supervised
Learning, Computer Vision, Natural Language Processing
I. I NTRODUCTION
Transformer networks [1] have attracted significant attention
in recent years due to their effectiveness in processing long-
range dependencies within data and their superior performance
across a wide range of tasks such as Computer Vision (CV)
and Natural Language Processing (NLP). Despite their empiri-
cal success, Transformer architectures rely heavily on manual
design by human experts, requiring extensive trial-and-error
experiments to determine optimal design choices.
Neural Architecture Search (NAS) [2] has emerged as a
promising approach to automate the design of neural networks.
Among various NAS methods, zero-shot approaches [3] [4]
have gained substantial attention. These methods employ zero-
shot proxies to estimate a network’s performance at the
initialization stage, enabling rapid evaluation within seconds
by computing statistics derived from a single forward or
backward propagation pass using a mini-batch of data [5].
By leveraging automated network design, such methods can
uncover custom architectures that match or exceed the per-
formance of manually designed Transformers in CV and NLP
tasks. However, most recent NAS research has been conducted
within fully supervised learning paradigms, where optimal
Transformer architectures are trained using both input data

introduces significant limitations, as it substantially increases

dataset required the efforts of 49,000 Amazon Mechanical

and cost-effective solutions. As a potential solution, self-

directly from the input data itself, has demonstrated consider-
able success in mitigating the dependence on labeled data [7]
[8]. Despite its promise, there has been limited research on
integrating zero-shot NAS with SSL.

II. R ELATED WORK
A. Neural Architecture Search
The earliest neural architecture search algorithms adopt
Reinforcement Learning [11] that evaluates the performance
of the models obtained through training. A complete search
process typically requires training hundreds or even thousands
of architectures, resulting in overall search times reaching
several thousand hours or more, which is extremely resource-
intensive and economically inefficient.
To address this problem, one-shot NAS [12] [13] uses the
concept of parameter sharing by training a large network.
During training, one-shot NAS selects and trains only one
subnet at a time from the large network. After training, the
weight parameters are placed back into the large network.
Although one-shot NAS reduces training computational load
and memory usage, the shared weight parameters among
subnets can cause mutual interference, making it challenging
to ensure that each subnet is fully trained, which can affect
model performance.
Zero-Shot NAS [3] [14] [15] [16] does not involve any train-
ing of architectures. Instead, it uses a performance predictor
to estimate the quality of the searched architectures, signifi-
cantly reducing search time and computational load. However,
earlier zero-shot NAS approaches face several issues. Firstly,
many zero-shot NAS performance predictors are typically
accurate only for a specific type of neural network [16]
[17]. Furthermore, many studies are applied to single tasks
like image classification and do not achieve cross-model or
cross-application generalization. Lastly, most zero-shot NAS
methods focus on the supervised learning framework, limiting
the potential to deploy these methods in real applications.
B. Self-Supervised Learning
Self-supervised learning (SSL) uses unlabeled data to learn
meaningful representations. SSL relies on pretext tasks that
create pseudo-labels from the data itself, helping models
generalize effectively to various downstream tasks. GPT-2 [18]
uses an autoregressive language modeling objective, predicting
the next token in a sequence based on the context. By training
on large text datasets, GPT-2 achieves excellent results on
various NLP tasks after fine-tuning on small labeled datasets.
With the development of vision transformers (ViTs) [19]
[20] [21], Masked Image Modeling (MIM) gradually replaces
the dominant position of contrastive learning [22] [23] in
visual self-supervised representation learning due to its su-
perior fine-tuning performance in various visual downstream
tasks. MAE [8] masks patches of an input image and trains
a vision transformer to reconstruct the missing pixel val-
ues. MCMAE [24] proposes a hybrid convolution-transformer
to learn more discriminative representations via the mask
auto-encoding scheme. MaskTAS [25] introduces a MIM-
based self-supervised one-shot NAS framework incorporating
knowledge distillation. However, the proposed method is not
evaluated in limited labeled data scenarios.
III. M ETHODOLOGY
A. Entropy Score
Generalization ability [26] refers to generalizing from the
training data to limited unseen data. A network with strong
generalization capability should achieve comparable perfor-
mance using fewer labeled data, indicating that it has learned
meaningful and transferable representations. Given the random
initialized weights and inputs, we evaluate the generalization
ability of a self-supervised network by the uniformity of
feature space. The isotropy of the feature space [27] refers
to how uniformly a model’s features are distributed across
different directions. Studies have shown that networks with
higher isotropy at initialization tend to demonstrate better
generalization ability [28]. To measure the isotropy of the
feature space, we analyze the eigenvalues of the feature matrix.
Let fk ∈ Rc×n be the self-attention feature matrix of the p-th
layer in a Transformer with H number of heads, where c is
the number of channels, n is the number of tokens. We use
singular value decomposition and square each singular value
to obtain c eigenvalues λ1 to λc of the feature matrix. If the
different orientations in the feature space are distributed more
isotropically, the magnitudes of the c eigenvalues become more
similar. Consequently, we use the entropy of the c eigenvalues
as a metric to evaluate the model’s generalization ability:

B. TPC Score
TPC-NAS [9] is a fast zero-shot NAS method. It evaluates
an architecture’s quality using expressivity [10]. A model with
higher architectural expressivity is expected to perform better
on practical tasks or applications. Consider a neuron in a
network as a node, where multiple paths connect it to nodes
in the next layer, each associated with a different weight. A
greater number of paths in a model can enable more diverse
transformations of the input data. As a result, architectures
with more paths generally exhibit better expressivity [10]. For
instance, the model in Figure 3 has a total end-to-end path
count of 81.

C. GEN-TPC-NAS Algorithms
GEN-TPC-NAS combines Entropy score and TPC score
with genetic algorithms. As illustrated in Algorithm 1 and
Figure 6, it begins with a population P of size N, initializing
each individual in the population with random architecture
parameters. Then it does Mutation: randomly select individuals
from P and apply mutation to generate new individuals or
Crossover: Use crossover techniques by randomly selecting
two individuals from the population and exchanging their
architecture parameters to create new individuals. If the newly
generated individuals don’t meet the constraints of parameter
Fig. 6. Overview of the search algorithm in GEN-TPC-NAS.
count or computational complexity, discard these individuals
and generate new ones. After N valid individuals are created,
we combine the new generation with the previous generation’s
individuals and select the top N individuals with the highest
TPC scores to form the next generation. Suppose the difference
between the maximum and minimum TPC scores among the
N individuals in population P is smaller than a predefined
threshold T0. In that case, it indicates that the N candidates
within the population exhibit no significant differences in their
TPC scores. At this point, the evaluation score is switched
from TPC score to Entropy score to assess the generalization
ability of the architectures. Additionally, a minimum TPC
Score is introduced as a new constraint during the search
process, signifying that the search aims to discover archi-
tectures with the highest generalization capability, provided
their expressivity exceeds a certain value. After sufficient
generations, the individual with the highest evaluation score
in the final population represents the best model architecture.
This approach maintains the fast search advantage of zero-
shot NAS by only using model architecture parameters and
one forward propagation, thus avoiding the need for extensive
training and keeping search time less than one hour.
D. Search Space Design
We take inspiration from [29] and design a search space
that includes four adjustable factors within Transformer build-
ing blocks: embedding dimension, Q-K-V dimension, head
dimension, and MLP ratio. The number of heads in the multi-
head attention module is determined by dividing the Q-K-V
dimension by the head dimension. Details of the search space
configurations for various Transformer architectures tailored
to CV and NLP tasks are provided in Table III. Notably,
we define the search range for the Q-K-V dimension to be
slightly larger than that of the embedding dimension, allowing
for greater flexibility in the number of attention heads. This
design enables each attention module to dynamically adjust
and determine the optimal number of heads as needed.
The constraints applied during the search process primarily
include the parameter count, computational complexity, and
the number of layers. While having more parameters in an
architecture can potentially improve both training and test

IV. E XPERIMENTS
In this section, we first provide the experimental settings
of the proposed GEN-TPC-NAS. Then we compare GEN-
TPC-NAS with the state-of-the-art models. Finally, we conduct
ablation studies on ViT models and GPT2. All the experiments
are conducted on 2 GeForce RTX 4090 GPUs.
A. Experimental settings
Dataset Description . For the computer vision task, we
evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method on the
large-scale ImageNet-1k [6] image classification and ADE20K
[36] semantic segmentation. Additionally, we use ImageNet-
100, comprising the first 100 classes from ImageNet-1k, for
ablation studies. For the NLP task, we utilize the Cleaned
Common Crawl dataset and the Cleaned Wikipedia dataset as
pre-training datasets. The test dataset used for fine-tuning is
WikiText-2, a curated collection of over 100 million tokens
extracted from verified Wikipedia articles.
Implementation Details . During the search phase, we
maintain a fixed population size of 100 and iterate through
1000 generations. The difference threshold T0 is set to 0.5. For
the computer vision task, we select DeiT-Tiny as the baseline
model for the challenging ImageNet-1k Experiments. The
design of the search space is detailed in Table III, while the
search constraints are determined by the original architecture’s
parameter size, computational cost, and depth.
In the pre-training stage, we employ the MaskTAS algo-
rithm [25] and train for 100 epochs. We use a batch size of
1024 and a base learning rate of 1.5e-4. During fine-tuning,
the entire dataset is used to train 200 epochs for the standard
ImageNet-1k benchmark. For the low-labeled data ImageNet-
1k benchmark, we fine-tune models for 200 epochs on 20–40%
of ImageNet-1k labeled data to evaluate model performance
under limited annotation conditions. For ADE20K, we follow
the same setting in MAE and adopt UperNet [37] as our
framework with a GEN-TPC-Tiny pre-trained backbone. The
input image resolution is 512 × 512 for training and evaluating.
We use mIoU as the evaluation metric.
For the NLP task, we adopt a 12-layer GPT-2 model with
110 million parameters as the baseline. The search space
design is shown in Table III, with constraints ensuring similar
computational and parameter sizes. We set the learning rate
to 6e-4 and the batch size to 6. We pre-train GPT2 with an

Scores. As a result, the selected architectures exhibit higher
Entropy scores but remain sub-optimal due to a gap in TPC
scores compared to the optimal architecture.

V. C ONCLUSION
In this study, we introduce GEN-TPC-NAS, a novel self-
supervised zero-shot neural architecture search (NAS) method
for Transformer architectures. By combining two zero-shot
proxies from complementary perspectives, our method identi-
fies architectures that effectively balance expressivity and gen-
eralization. The integration of NAS and self-supervised learn-

ing (SSL) not only significantly reduces the annotation costs
of supervised learning, but also facilitates the efficient training
of transformers. Experimental results on image classification
and text generation tasks demonstrate that GEN-TPC-NAS can

labeled data scenarios. These findings highlight the potential
of GEN-TPC-NAS to automate the neural architecture design
process for real-world applications, reducing dependency on
human expertise and lowering data labeling costs.
