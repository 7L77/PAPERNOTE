  202: windows Transformer (Swin Transformer) [26] regulates the stage compute ratio as 1 : 1 : 3 :
  203: 1 or 1 : 1 : 9 : 1. On the CNN side, ConvNeXt [ 27] reshuf铿俥s depths in all stages according to

  205: linear parameterization to assign network depth and width. As network depth and width are

  272: 4.2 Convolutional over Patchify stem stage
  273: As a preprocessor, a common stem stage 鈥渁ggressively鈥?downsamples input images due to
  274: the redundancy inherent in natural images. The stem stage in ResNet-50 [ 15] consists of a
  275: stride-two 7 鈬?7 convolution and a stride-two max-pooling. Built on ResNet-50, RegNet [ 36]
  276: replaces the max-pooling with a stride-two residual shortcut in the 铿乺st block of the 铿乺st body
  277: stage, dubbed postponed downsampling. On the Transformer side, the stem stage patchi铿乪s
  278: the image into p 鈬?p non-overlapping patches. It is implemented by a stride- pp 鈬?p convolu-
  279: tion, with p = 14/16 in vision Transformer (ViT) [11], and p = 4 in Swin Transformer [ 26].
  280: Both patchify stem and ResNet-style convolutional stem are applicable to CNNs and Trans-
  281: formers. As a pure CNN, ConvNeXt [ 27] borrowed the patchify stem from Transformer, and
  282: Xiao et al.[ 49] successfully employed the convolutional stem in ViT.
  283: Inspired by these 铿乶dings, we compare how patchify and convolutional stem affect ad-
  284: versarial robustness. We set p = 4 following Swin Transformer and ConvNeXt for patchify
  285: stem (Patch 4, Stride 4) and use postponed downsampling for convolutional stem. From the
  286: results in Fig. 2b, we observe both designs show better robustness than the baseline ResNet-
  287: 50, but postponed downsampling is signi铿乧antly higher than patchify stem. There are two
  288: differences between these two designs: a smaller stride and the overlapping region between
  289: two convolution kernels in the convolutional stem. We verify whether these two distinctions
  290: are bene铿乧ial to robustness. First, we reduce the patch size from 4 鈬?4 to 2 鈬?2, and add the
  291: stride-two residual shortcut as in postponed downsampling to maintain the total downsam-
  292: pling ratio. This modi铿乧ation (Patch 2, Stride 2) improves PGD 10-4 and clean accuracies of
  293: 4 鈬?4 patch by 0.23 and 0.98 pp. Next, we gradually increase the overlapping area between
  294: the 4 鈬?4 patch by decreasing the stride from 3 to 1. We observe a consistent increment while
  295: decreasing the stride, and the 4 鈬?4 patch with the largest overlapping between neighboring
  296: patches (Patch 4, Stride 1) performs almost on par with the postponed downsampling. Fi-
  297: nally, we additionally experiment on different output channel widths since it barely changes
  298: S. PENG ET AL.: ROBUST ARCHITECTURAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES 7
  299: total parameters. Decreasing the width from 64 (ResNet-50) to 32 lowers the accuracy due to
  300: fewer model parameters. However, increasing the width from 64 to 96 boosts PGD 10-4 and
  301: clean accuracies by 1.47 and 1.24 pp with a negligible 0.01M increase in total parameters. In
  302: summary, the convolution stem with postponed downsampling design outperforms patchify
  303: stem due to its less aggressive stride-two downsampling and overlapped convolution kernels.
  304: Besides, widening the output channels signi铿乧antly improves robustness almost at no cost.
  305: 4.3 Robust Residual Block Design
  306: 4.3.1 Squeeze & Excitation
  307: Squeeze and excitation (SE) block is a simple but effective add-on for the original residual
  308: block. It was proposed to adaptively recalibrate channel-wise feature responses by modeling
  309: interdependencies between channels [ 18]. RegNet [ 36] also veri铿乪s the effectiveness of SE
  310: on improving clean accuracy. However, Huang et al.[ 20] found that a straightforward appli-
  311: cation of SE hurts robustness on CIFAR-10, and added an extra skip connection around the
  312: SE. Driven by the discrepancy in clean and adversarial accuracies, we examine the SE block
  313: with ResNet-50. Note that the SE block will introduce additional parameters. Thus, we fol-
  314: low RegNet that sets r = 4 and appends the SE block directly after the 3 鈬?3 convolutional
  315: layer since it possesses fewer channels. Our experiments on ImageNet show a different pic-
  316: ture, where the original SE block markedly increases all PGD and clean accuracies compared
  317: to ResNet-50 (Fig. 2b). We hypothesize that the different effects on CIFAR and ImageNet
  318: are caused by the reduction ratio r since Huang et al. only tested on r = 16. To provide a fair
  319: comparison, we train WRN with a sweep of hyperparameter r = {2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64}. Our ex-
  320: periments show that adversarial robustness is negatively correlated with r, and when r   32,
  321: the accuracy is inferior to the baseline WRN. More details are in supplementary material
  322: Sec. B.2. Therefore, SE block is a uni铿乪d architecture component that improves both clean
  323: and adversarial accuracies, and we set r = 4 in all designs that include the SE block.
  324: 4.3.2 Non-parametric Smooth Activation Functions
  325: Both the number and the function of activation layers are different between natural language
  326: processing (NLP) and vision architectures. In terms of the number, Transformer [ 11] only
  327: has one activation in the multilayer perceptron (MLP) block. In comparison, the activation
  328: is normally appended to all convolutions in a CNN block [ 15, 16]. ConvNeXt [ 27] 铿乺st
  329: observed this phenomenon and reduced the total activations from three to one in all blocks,
  330: which leads to higher clean accuracy. Inspired by ConvNeXt, We combinatorially analyzed
  331: all possible locations of the activation in a single block. The average PGD 10-4 accuracies
  332: are 30.43%, 29.00%, and 24.84% when total activations are set to 3, 2, and 1, which are
  333: all inferior to ResNet-50. Thus, reducing activation layers is not bene铿乧ial to adversarial
  334: robustness, and we preserve the activation along with all convolutions.
  335: In terms of the activation function, it is common practice to use ReLU in CNNs [ 15]
  336: due to its ef铿乧iency and simplicity. However, advanced Transformers, e.g., BERT [8], GPT-
  337: 2[ 35], and hierarchical Transformers [25] adopted smoother variants of ReLU, e.g., gaussian
  338: error linear unit (GELU) [ 17] and sigmoid linear unit (SiLU) [ 12]. Recent research shows
  339: replacing ReLU with its smooth approximations facilitates better gradient updates and leads
  340: to higher robustness [51]. For example, solely replacing all ReLUs with GELUs signi铿乧antly
  341: improves the PGD accuracy of a standard ResNet-50 [ 2]. Dai et al.[ 5] proposed paramet-
  342: 8 S. PENG ET AL.: ROBUST ARCHITECTURAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES
  343: ric activation functions by adding learnable parameters to non-parametric versions, which
  344: showed mixed performance on CIFAR-10 for different design spaces. Compared to SiLU,
  345: parametric SiLU (PSiLU) and parametric shifted SiLU (PSSiLU) show higher robustness
  346: when applied to WRN-28-10, but show degraded performance when applied to ResNet-18.
  347: Therefore, we examine and compare the robustness gain brought by GELU, SiLU, paramet-
  348: ric ReLU (PReLU), PSiLU, and PSSiLU. We 铿乶d that: (1) non-parametric smooth activa-
  349: tions, e.g., SiLU and GELU are signi铿乧antly higher than ReLU (ResNet-50); and (2) for
  350: parametric activations, PReLU performs on par with ReLU, and both PSiLU and PSSiLU
  351: are inferior to SiLU. Synthesizing all the above 铿乶dings, we recommend non-parametric
  352: smooth activations due to consistency and simplicity, thus replacing all ReLUs with SiLUs.

  366: 5 Adversarial Robustness Evaluation
  367: We have completed the exploration of how individual architectural components affect adver-
  368: sarial robustness. It is encouraging to uncover how these components affect robustness, but it
  369: is not completely convincing unless the following two questions are addressed: 1) Can these
  370: components consistently improve robustness when grouped together? 2) Are these design
  371: principles generalizable? Sec. 5.1 provides a roadmap that robusti铿乪s a CNN with all three
  372: design principles, dubbed Robust architecture (Ra). For a model robusti铿乪d with our design
  373: principles, we tag it with Ra . Then, we scale model architectures under diverse parameter
  374: budgets, extrapolate to the design spaces of ResNet [15] and WRN [53], train all models with
  375: various A T methods, and evaluate on CIFAR (Sec. 5.2) and ImageNet (Sec. 5.3) to verify
  376: the generalization of our design principles. Full comparisons with CNNs, Transformers, and
  377: NAS-based architectures are provided in supplementary materials Sec. C and D.

  450: 6 Conclusion
  451: We synthesize a suite of three generalizable robust architectural design principles: (a) opti-
  452: mal range for depth and width con铿乬urations, (b) preferring convolutional over patchify stem
  453: stage, and (c) robust residual block design through adopting squeeze and excitation blocks
  454: and non-parametric smooth activation functions. Through extensive experiments across a
  455: wide spectrum of dataset scales, adversarial training methods , model parameters, and net-
  456: work design spaces, our principles consistently and markedly improve adversarial robustness
  457: on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet.
